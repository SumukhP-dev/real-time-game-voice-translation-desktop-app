### Funding goal (demand-focused)
**Goal amount:** `$1,000`–`$2,500` (or similar — keep it low to reflect *demand*, not revenue)

- **Why a low goal:** The campaign is about **validating that players want this**, not raising a target revenue. A lower goal is easier to hit, so the project gets funded and you prove demand; anything above the goal is bonus.  
- All-or-nothing: you only get funds if you meet the goal, so the number should feel achievable with your expected audience.  
- **In your story/copy:** Say something like: *“Our goal isn’t to fund development—the app is already built. We’re raising a small amount to show that players want real-time voice translation in their games. If we hit our goal, we’re shipping to every backer.”*  
- Use Kickstarter’s calculator to see fees on your chosen number; keep the goal minimal so success = demand validated.

---

### Recommended ad spend (e.g. Reddit Ads)

With a **$1,000–$2,500** goal, keep paid ads disciplined so you don't spend more than a sensible share of what you're raising.

- **Daily budget:** Start with **$10–$20/day** for 3–5 days to get real CPC and click-through data. Avoid jumping straight to $50/day until you know Reddit is driving Kickstarter visits (or backers).
- **Total campaign budget:** Aim for **~$200–$500 total** for the full campaign (e.g. 30 days). That's roughly 10–20% of a $2.5k goal — enough to test and learn without risking more than you're comfortable losing.
- **When to spend more:** If cost per click is reasonable (e.g. under $1–$2) and you see Kickstarter referrals or backers from Reddit, you can increase to $25–$35/day. If clicks are expensive or don't convert, improve targeting and creative before increasing spend.
- **When to spend less:** If you're over $2–$3 per click or getting few Kickstarter visits, reduce daily budget and refine audiences (e.g. only gaming subreddits) and ad creative first.

**Summary:** Start small ($10–$20/day), cap total ad spend around $200–$500 for the campaign, and scale up only if the numbers justify it.

## If the goal isn't funded

Not hitting the goal doesn't automatically mean "no one wants this." Kickstarter favors physical and creative projects; digital-only software often gets less organic traffic. Consider the following before pivoting the product.

**1. Iterate before you pivot**

- **Relaunch (optional):** Try once more with a lower goal (e.g. $500–$750), stronger video, and tighter targeting (gaming subreddits, Discord, streamers). Some projects succeed on a second run.
- **Sell elsewhere:** The app is already built. List it on **itch.io**, **Gumroad**, or your own site at the same price. Many indie tools validate and sell better there than on Kickstarter. Use the same messaging ("Break the language barrier in voice chat") and track sales and feedback.
- **Free/demo tier:** Offer a limited free version or trial to get installs and word-of-mouth; convert to paid with a license. That validates demand with real usage, not just pledges.

**2. When a channel pivot makes sense**

If you get **meaningful sales or interest** on itch.io/Gumroad/Discord/Reddit, the product is validated—Kickstarter was just the wrong channel. Keep the product; double down on where it works (SEO, community, streamer outreach).

If you get **almost no interest** anywhere (no clicks, no wishlists, no sales after a few months of effort), then the issue may be messaging, discoverability, or a smaller market than expected—not necessarily the product idea itself.

**3. When a product pivot might be worth it**

Consider a real pivot (e.g. different use case, audience, or packaging) only **after** you've tried:

- Selling on itch.io / Gumroad for at least a few months with clear pricing and the same positioning.
- Reaching the right audience (gaming subs, Discord servers, streamers, esports/coaching communities).

If you've done that and still see no demand, then options include: narrowing to one game or one audience (e.g. "CS2 only" or "for streamers"), adding a B2B angle (teams, leagues), or exploring adjacent problems (e.g. accessibility, language learning). That's a product pivot. Until then, **pivot the channel and the campaign, not the product.**

**Summary:** Try direct sales and a possible Kickstarter relaunch before changing what the app does. Only consider a product pivot if other channels also show no traction after a sustained push.
